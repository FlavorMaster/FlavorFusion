FRANCE

Boeuf Bourguignon

a traditional French stew made with beef braised in red wine, typically Burgundy, along with beef broth. The dish is flavored with garlic, onions, and mushrooms, and often includes carrots and pearl onions. Slow-cooked to tender perfection, it is rich and hearty, with a deep, complex flavor enhanced by the wine and aromatic vegetables. It's typically served with potatoes, noodles, or crusty bread to soak up the savory sauce.

Bouillabaissse

a traditional Provençal fish stew originating from the port city of Marseille, France. It features a variety of Mediterranean fish and shellfish, simmered with aromatic herbs, fennel, tomatoes, leeks, onions, and garlic. Saffron and orange zest add distinctive flavors. The broth is rich and flavorful, often served with a side of rouille (a garlicky mayonnaise) and crusty bread. This hearty, aromatic stew embodies the essence of coastal French cuisine.

Chicken Confit

a French dish where chicken legs are slowly cooked in their own fat until tender and flavorful. The process involves curing the chicken with salt, garlic, and herbs (such as thyme and bay leaves) for several hours or overnight, then cooking it gently in rendered chicken fat at a low temperature. The result is incredibly moist, flavorful, and tender meat with a crispy skin when finished in the oven. Chicken confit can be served with roasted vegetables, potatoes, or a fresh salad.

Croque Monsieur

a classic French ham and cheese sandwich, typically made with ham, Gruyère or Emmental cheese, and béchamel sauce, all layered between slices of buttered bread. The sandwich is then topped with more cheese and either broiled or baked until the cheese is melted and golden brown. It is a rich and indulgent dish, often enjoyed as a café or bistro staple. When topped with a fried or poached egg, it becomes a Croque Madame.

Frangipane Tart

a classic French pastry featuring a sweet almond filling known as frangipane, made from ground almonds, butter, sugar, and eggs. The tart typically has a buttery, flaky crust that holds the rich and creamy frangipane filling. Often, it's topped with sliced fruits like pears, apples, or berries, which add a delicious contrast to the nutty sweetness of the filling. The tart is baked until golden brown and can be served warm or at room temperature, often dusted with powdered sugar or glazed with apricot jam for added shine.

French Onion Soup

A classic French dish made with caramelized onions, beef broth, white wine, and a hint of thyme. The soup is simmered until rich and flavorful, then traditionally served in bowls topped with toasted baguette slices and melted Gruyère or Swiss cheese. The cheese is broiled until bubbly and golden brown, creating a deliciously gooey topping that pairs perfectly with the savory, sweet onions. This comforting soup is often enjoyed as a starter or a main course, especially during colder months.

Hazelnut Darcqoise

a classic French dessert consisting of layers of hazelnut meringue, typically filled with rich buttercream, ganache, or whipped cream. The meringue layers are made from egg whites, sugar, and finely ground hazelnuts, baked until crisp and golden. Once assembled, the dessert is often garnished with additional hazelnuts, chocolate shavings, or powdered sugar. The combination of the crunchy meringue and creamy filling creates a delightful texture and a rich, nutty flavor that is both elegant and indulgent.

lamb shank navarin

A classic French stew made with lamb shanks and a variety of spring vegetables. The dish gets its name from "navet," the French word for turnip, which is a key ingredient. The lamb shanks are browned and then slow-cooked in a rich broth of wine, stock, tomatoes, and aromatics such as garlic and herbs. The stew also includes vegetables like carrots, potatoes, and peas. The slow cooking process makes the lamb tender and infuses the dish with deep, savory flavors. Lamb Shank Navarin is typically served with crusty bread or over a bed of mashed potatoes or rice.

quiche lorraine

a classic French tart made with a savory custard filling of eggs, cream, and bacon or lardons, baked in a buttery, flaky pie crust. Often, it includes grated Gruyère or Emmental cheese for added richness and flavor. The filling is poured into the pre-baked crust and baked until the custard is set and the top is golden brown. This dish is typically served warm or at room temperature and is perfect for breakfast, brunch, or lunch, often accompanied by a simple green salad.
